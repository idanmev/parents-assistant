# the child — What Actually Works (Evidence From Journals)

**Last updated:** 2026-05-09  
**Source:** All journal entries historical data, confirmed working techniques only

This file contains techniques that have been observed working with the child specifically. Not general ADHD advice — actual journal-confirmed results.

## Transition Tools

### Timer + 5-Minute Warning (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "the child, let's give ourselves 5 more minutes to eat, it's been too long." the child agreed, used timer. Worked immediately.  
**How to use:** State duration as fact ("5 more minutes"), set visible timer, don't renegotiate when it ends.

### Secret Sign Between Parent-Child (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study — After timer, Parent 1 said "let's make a sign." They created a shared secret sign. Turned the situation from heavy to playful cooperation.  
**How to use:** Create the sign at a calm moment, not mid-conflict. It gives the child an inside-joke relationship with the rule.

### "Let's Hide From Mom" / Playful Reframe (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "Let's hide from Mom and go shower — Mom doesn't know our sign." the child flowed with it and went genuinely.  
**How to use:** Reframe the transition as a game or mission. Works best with Parent 1. Parent 2 can adapt with her own version. The key: make it feel like their idea, not a command.

### Advance Warning × Multiple Rounds (★★★★☆)
**Evidence:** Dec 4 — "Called her to leave for class several times in advance to prepare her. When finally called — she came out without resistance."  
**How to use:** Start warnings 15 min before, then 10, then 5, then "now." Never just "now" with no lead-up.

### Croissant / Snack as Transition Bridge (★★★★☆)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "When leaving, Parent 2 gave 5 more minutes, then used a croissant as a gentle transition tool — it worked."  
**How to use:** Pair the end of one activity with the start of something pleasurable (snack, special drink, activity). She moves toward, not away from.

## Morning Tools

### One Step at a Time, Stated as Fact (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Dec 4 — "Gave her one task at a time (brush teeth, get dressed, etc.) — same order every day, so the child already starts it automatically."  
**How to use:** "the child, first teeth. ✓ Now shirt. ✓ Now shoes." Never layer instructions. Never ask "do you want to?"

### Physical Help with Dressing (★★★★☆)
**Evidence:** Dec 4 — "Parent 2 helps physically with dressing."  
**How to use:** Offering help is not regression. At ADHD morning-brain-load, the act of choosing + initiating is too heavy. Help reduces load, keeps the morning moving.

### Naming Her Feeling = Half the Load Drops (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "After a few minutes the child could say she didn't want to go — she was tired, AND because yesterday her friend felt homesick. Once she said 'I'm tired and want to be home' — half the load dropped."  
**How to use:** When she erupts, don't push. Create space for her to say what's wrong. Once named → she can move. Ask: "It seems like something is heavy today. What's going on?"

### YouTube as Post-Cooperation Reward (★★★☆☆)
**Evidence:** Case Study — Parent 2 gave YouTube only after the child found strength to cooperate. the child chose art content, closed phone with no problem when time was up.  
**How to use:** ONLY after cooperation, not as bribe. "You showed so much strength this morning. One YouTube as a special thing." Reinforce the cooperation, then give it.

## Emotional Regulation Tools

### Breathing Out Loud (Parent Models, Child Joins) (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "Parent 2 forgot something important, said she was irritated. Realized she needs to breathe — said out loud 'I'm going to breathe' and asked if the child wants to join. the child said okay. Parent 2 breathed audibly so the child could hear. The atmosphere calmed."  
**How to use:** Parent says out loud "I need to breathe right now" and does it audibly. the child will join. This works because she mirrors regulated adults — it's not a tool you use ON her, it's something you do FOR yourself that she copies.

### Reflecting the Feeling Without Rushing to Fix (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study, Nov 15, Case Study — Multiple entries where Parent 2 sitting quietly and reflecting ("you seem sad") → the child opened up.  
**How to use:** Sit at her level. "I see this is really hard." Wait. Don't add instructions, solutions, or corrections for at least 60 seconds. The reflection itself is the intervention.

### Space + Return (Dual Approach) (★★★★☆)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "Both gave her space alone and tried talking. Parent 2 approached calmly, said she probably feels sad and wants more family time."  
**How to use:** Give space when she needs to explode. But return — don't leave her alone for too long. Balance space with presence.

### Parent 2 Stepping Away to Calm Herself (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "Parent 2 stepped inside for a moment to calm down — felt really angry." Then returned calmly. the child cooperation improved.  
**How to use:** Parent leaving to regulate themselves is not abandonment — it's modeling. Say it out loud: "I need one minute" then come back. This is more effective than trying to regulate while already reactive.

## Bedtime Tools

### 5-Min Talk + 5-Min Silliness + Story + Sleep Music (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "5 minutes of talking + 5 minutes of silliness (tickles + dances) + story + sleep music. She fell asleep beautifully."  
**How to use:** This sequence reliably works. Don't skip steps. The silliness (tickles, dancing) is not optional — it burns off final energy and creates positive physical connection before sleep.

### Holding "No More" with Playfulness (★★★★☆)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "When story ended, she asked for more. Parent 1 said 'no more' and held that with playfulness. Parent 2 reinforced him. the child fell asleep beautifully."  
**How to use:** The limit at story-end must be held. But hold it lightly — "no more stories tonight, that was a beautiful one." No negotiation, no escalation. If she pushes, repeat the same phrase once, then go quiet.

### Consistent Parent (Don't Keep Swapping) (★★★★☆)
**Evidence:** Case Study — Parent 2 took over from Parent 1 at bedtime; transition was managed. But swapping mid-bedtime routine increases resistance.  
**How to use:** Decide who does bedtime before starting. If swap is needed, brief handoff with the child present: "Dad is tired, Mom is finishing with you tonight."

## Trust-Building Tools

### Keep Promises (★★★★★)
**Evidence:** Case Study — Coffee shop promise → kept → the child returned to cooperation.  
**Natascha's explanation:** "When you keep a promise, the child feels 'Mom remembers me. What I feel is important. I can trust her.'" Trust = behavioral stability. Broken promises cause outsized distress.  
**How to use:** Only promise what you can deliver. Small kept promises > large broken ones.

### Chalkboard / Visual Countdown (★★★★☆)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "Bought a chalkboard to count the days until Dad returns." the child engaged with this and calmed about the absence.  
**How to use:** Any predictable uncertainty (Dad trip, school event, visit) → make it visual and countable. "3 days, then Dad. Let's mark it." This converts an abstract stressor into a manageable timeline.

### Surprise in Lunchbox (★★★★☆)
**Evidence:** Case Study — "Small note from them and a small chocolate — the parent's idea. Shows her how much you love her unconditionally, even when it's hard."  
**How to use:** After a hard morning, a note in the lunchbox turns the day. Not as bribe — as unconditional expression. "We love you even when it was hard this morning."

## What DOES NOT Work (Do Not Recommend)

| Approach | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Open questions when exhausted ("Do you want to...?") | Creates impossible choice-load → meltdown |
| Sand timer for morning (Case Study) | the child's brain not "lit up" yet — external tools don't register |
| Explaining/reasoning during meltdown | Prefrontal offline — she cannot process |
| "Why did you do that?" after incident | Adds shame without teaching anything |
| Swapping rules based on mood/tiredness | Inconsistency = the child must re-test every time where the boundary is |
| Morning neighbor play | Disrupts the only predictable structure of the hardest transition |
| Giving YouTube as pre-cooperation bribe | Confirms meltdown = reward; opposite of what's needed |
| Stacking multiple simultaneous changes | Research: never new country + new school + new activity at same time |
