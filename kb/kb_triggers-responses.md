# Maya — Trigger Map & Response Protocols

**Last updated:** 2026-05-09  
**Source:** All journal entries Nov–Dec 2025 + Natascha coaching + research synthesis

---

## The Universal Response Sequence

**For any escalation, always:**
1. **Regulate first** — name the feeling, validate it ("I see you're overwhelmed")
2. **Don't add demands** — zero new instructions while amygdala is activated
3. **One small step** — after partial calm, give exactly one concrete instruction
4. **Immediate specific praise** — reinforce any movement toward cooperation
5. **Review later** — after full calm (hours), briefly revisit what happened; never in the moment

**The formula Natascha uses:**
> Empathy + calm limit + small step = stability and dramatic drop in resistance

## Trigger 1: Morning (Highest Difficulty)

**Why:** Home→school is the most cognitively demanding transition of the day. Tired brain + unfamiliar environment demands + executive function overload.

**What you'll see:**
- Slowness, non-responsiveness
- Eruption when pushed to move faster
- "I don't want to go to school"
- Refusing to dress, brush teeth, eat

**Response protocol:**
- Less talking, less discussing — more clear leading
- One step at a time, stated as fact not question: "Maya, first socks, then shirt, I'll help you"
- NEVER: "Do you want to get dressed?" (open question = impossible task when exhausted)
- Physical help with dressing is fine and age-appropriate for ADHD
- If she names a feeling ("I'm tired, I want to be home") → validate immediately, then move: "I hear you, and we're going forward together — first shoes"
- Morning neighbor play = FIXED RULE: not allowed. "In the morning we only say hi"
- One-time YouTube exception = only AFTER cooperation, never as a bribe beforehand

**Timing context:** If she mentions a peer issue from yesterday (friend went home sick, fight with classmate) → this is the real source of resistance. Address it: "It sounds like yesterday was hard because of [X]. You can tell me more in the car."

## Trigger 2: Transitions (Activity Ending)

**Why:** Requires simultaneous inhibition + mental set-shifting + working memory + emotion regulation + initiation. ADHD impairs all five.

**Highest-risk transition points:**
- End of markers/drawing session
- End of screen time (YouTube, TV episode)
- Play with neighbor → coming inside
- Dinner → shower
- Shower → bedtime prep
- Bedtime story ending

**Response protocol:**
- Advance warning: "In 5 minutes we'll [X]" — given 2–3 times
- Visual anchor: timer or verbal countdown
- Playful reframe: "Let's do a secret mission to the bathroom" / "Let's hide from Mom and go shower"
- Transition bridge: physical object (croissant, snack) or activity she moves toward, not away from
- Never: "Time's up, stop now" with no warning
- Dinner → shower Idan method: timer + secret sign between them → worked immediately
- Hold the limit with playfulness, not escalation

## Trigger 3: Exhaustion + Choice Demand

**Why:** An exhausted ADHD brain cannot generate options. The decision-demand creates a cognitive jam → escalates to the only exit available: meltdown.

**Looks like:** Ice cream flavor conflict, clothes choice conflict, activity selection conflict

**Response protocol:**
- Pre-limit before the situation: "We're getting ice cream, you choose ONE flavor today" — said before entering, not in the middle
- Binary only: "Blue shirt or green shirt" — never open choice
- If already in meltdown over choice: remove the choice entirely ("I'll choose for you today, that's fine") 
- Don't match her escalation about the choice — stay flat and calm
- After she calms and gets something: she usually shares or softens → reinforce immediately

## Trigger 4: Parent Unavailability (Yonatan, Work Trips)

**Why:** Loss of co-regulation support = ADHD nervous system without its external scaffolding. Not jealousy in the simple sense — actual regulatory need.

**Looks like:**
- Interrupting Yonatan's bedtime repeatedly
- Demanding attention during feeding
- Increased meltdowns when Sveta is solo with both
- Idan's absence → more morning resistance

**Response protocol:**
- Pre-announce Yonatan bedtime routine: "While I put Yonatan to sleep, you watch your episode. When I come out, we do X together"
- Give her the schedule: she should know what she does during, and what comes after
- One predictable daily 1:1 moment (coffee shop, balcony dinner, 5 min talk before sleep) acts as buffer
- When saying "I can't right now — Yonatan": pair with concrete timer/promise "In 10 minutes when he's asleep, I'm all yours"
- Idan away = Sveta solo: prepare her the day before, "Dad comes back Thursday, let's mark it on the chalkboard"

## Trigger 5: Perceived Rejection / Peer Issues

**Why:** ADHD + RSD — social rejection registers as physical threat. Peer problems carry over into home behavior.

**Looks like:**
- Wanting to leave a place when an unwanted peer arrives
- School reluctance when a friend issue happened the day before
- Tearful "nobody likes me" after mild correction
- Refusing activities where she might fail or be judged

**Response protocol:**
- Don't dismiss: "You probably feel [X] because [Y happened]" — name it
- Don't force social exposure in flooded state: if she wants to leave the café, leave
- Later, in calm: "I noticed you wanted to leave when [classmate] came. What was that about?"
- For school reluctance: "Yesterday was hard because [X]. What if we talk about it on the way?"
- Pre-social events: brief prep "There might be kids you don't know. That's okay, we'll find your spot"

## Trigger 6: Sensory Discomfort

**Why:** ADHD high sensory sensitivity. "Slightly wet" = genuinely intolerable. "This smell" = real sensory pain. Not manipulation.

**Looks like:**
- Refusing damp/wet clothing ("it's wet!" even if barely damp)
- Screaming when parent touches food/fruit without washing hands
- Overwhelm in loud/crowded environments
- Physical aggression when personal space invaded

**Response protocol:**
- Believe the sensory report — never "that's not really wet/loud/etc."
- Pre-announce sensory changes: "I'm opening a fruit now, I'll eat at the table and wash hands right after"
- Clothing: always have a backup option. Wet = swap, don't negotiate
- If she's overwhelmed in an environment: trust her read, offer an exit or sensory buffer (quieter spot, headphones)
- When she says "wash your hands" in a firm tone: not disrespect, a genuine sensory need. Respond: "Good that you told me, washing now"

## Trigger 7: Boundary Testing Through the Body

**Why:** Physical limit-testing (hands in food, splashing, pushing, wiping hands on parent) = proximity-seeking and connection bid. "Are you with me? Do you still like me?"

**Looks like:**
- Hands in food/cup during dinner
- Splashing water during shower
- Wiping dirty hands on parent
- Rough touching of Yonatan

**Response protocol:**
- During dinner: NOT reacting to minor body-limit testing → allows her to eat and self-regulate
- For physical contact: "That doesn't feel good to me. I want to stay with you — soft hands, or we stop"
- Always: "Not [X], yes [Y]" — never just no: "No hands in the cup. You can stir with this spoon"
- After physical escalation: brief pause (step away), return: "I'm back. I want to be with you. What can we do together?"
- Shower: provide bath toys as alternative; "play with the toys, not the water guns at me"

## Trigger 8: End of Day Accumulation

**Why:** By evening, executive function is depleted. Each challenge across the day compounds. The child who cooperated at 9am may be completely dysregulated by 7pm.

**Evening response strategy:**
- Lower demands proportionally to how hard the day was
- If hard day: reduce evening screen time end-fight, be flexible on one thing
- Do NOT try to do makeup-work (extra homework, extra conversation about behavior) in the evening
- Evening review of schedule: "Here's what we do tonight: [X, Y, Z]" — she repeats it back → anchors her
- Bedtime: predictable structure (talk + silliness + story + music) matters more than exact timing
