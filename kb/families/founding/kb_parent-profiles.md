## Idan (Age 37)

### ADHD Status
**Diagnosed adult ADHD.** This is a critical factor. Idan's own ADHD directly affects:
- His ability to remember events (poor working memory under load)
- His consistency during high-stress periods
- His capacity to apply coaching strategies when depleted
- His emotional reactivity threshold

Research: Parental ADHD reduces engagement with and benefit from parent training. Idan needs the same kind of external scaffolding that Maya needs.

### Strengths
- **Playful creativity under calm conditions** — secret signs, "let's hide from Mom," gamified transitions, timer + reward systems. When he's fresh, his ideas are excellent and Maya loves them.
- **Emotional attunement** — notices Maya's cues when paying attention. Identified limit-testing as connection-seeking (Nov 13 entry)
- **Repair-oriented** — after the wet-pants incident (Nov 15), sat down at a café the next day, calmly explained, apologized, and worked through it with Maya. Maya learned what real apology looks like.
- **Willing to be vulnerable** — writes honestly about what he doesn't remember, what exhausts him. This openness makes coaching possible.
- **Physical play capacity** — tickles, dances, physical silliness at bedtime. Maya loves this from him specifically.

### Challenges
- **Over-functioning crash cycle** — extended work trips (Thailand off-site) → depleted → returns → can't parent well → frustration
- **Working memory failure** — "genuinely can't remember details despite recording after the fact" (Nov 27 entry). This is ADHD, not indifference.
- **Low frustration threshold when depleted** — wet pants incident (threw pants in frustration). His own ADHD triggers faster when tired/stressed.
- **Consistency dips under load** — strategies work when he has bandwidth; slip when he doesn't

### Natascha's Tool for Idan: "3 Moments a Day"
> "You don't need to remember the whole evening — that's impossible even for people without ADHD. Instead, at the end of the day write only: What worked? What was challenging? What do you want to try tomorrow? Two lines per point. This calms the brain, organizes the day, and gives a sense of control."

**Bot application:** When Idan writes the evening journal entry, prompt him with exactly these 3 questions. Don't ask for a narrative. Three short answers is the target.

### What Idan Needs from the Bot
- **Structure for memory** — the journal prompt should guide him, not require free recall
- **Affirmation of ADHD context** — when he forgets or inconsistency happens, acknowledge it's the ADHD, not failure
- **Short specific coaching** — he benefits from "try this one thing tomorrow" not long frameworks
- **Credit for creative wins** — when his playful approaches work, name them explicitly

## Sveta (Age 38)

### ADHD Status
**No ADHD.** Neurotypical. But carries the larger parenting load (especially during Idan's trips) with two children: Maya + Yonatan (baby).

### Strengths
- **Emotional attunement** — reads Maya's body language with precision. Notices "a certain look" that tells her Maya needs her and acts on it (Dec 6 entry).
- **Consistent empathy** — naturally uses reflection and validation. "You seem sad and tired, and you want to be home" — said spontaneously without coaching prompts.
- **Promise-keeping** — always follows through on commitments (coffee shop promise, picking up early). Maya trusts her word completely.
- **Regulatory modeling** — the Dec 2 breathing-in-the-car moment was exceptional and completely spontaneous: said out loud "I'm going to breathe," Maya joined, atmosphere changed.
- **Flexible authority** — knows when to hold firm and when to adapt. Ice cream conflict (Dec 4): held the one-flavor limit, offered supermarket alternative. Maya accepted.
- **Endurance** — managed both kids solo for 5 days (Idan trip Dec 2–6) with mostly positive outcomes across all entries.

### Challenges
- **Anger when overloaded** — "I was really angry" (Dec 2). Yonatan taking longer than expected + Maya interrupting = Sveta escalates. Knows it happens; working on catching it earlier.
- **Consistency under exhaustion** — "When I have energy I try to contain, explain, offer time. When I'm tired or under pressure I can get irritated back" (intake questionnaire). Honest self-assessment.
- **Yielding to pressure** — "In most cases she says 'I don't want to' and I tend to give in. If I insist she resists strongly but sometimes cooperates after a little persuasion" (intake). Working on holding limits more consistently.

### Natascha's Coaching for Sveta
- **Unified front:** Don't contradict Idan in front of Maya. If disagreeing → shoulder touch as signal, take over smoothly. Process disagreements after Maya is asleep.
- **Steps away = good parenting:** Stepping away to calm down is emotion regulation modeling, not abandonment.
- **"I was upset, it wasn't about you" (to Maya):** Taking responsibility after losing patience. Dec 6 entry: did this, then they talked quietly and Maya fell asleep peacefully. Natascha praised this specifically.
- **Sveta's empathy is a strength but needs paired limits:** "Excellent that you validated — and after the validation, move immediately to clear leading. Otherwise she stays inside the feeling."

### What Sveta Needs from the Bot
- **Reinforcement of her natural strengths** — she often doubts herself; her instincts are usually right
- **Help with Idan-alignment** — when Idan is inconsistent or absent, she needs coaching on how to bridge
- **SOS support during solo days** — when she's managing both kids alone, she needs quick real-time guidance
- **Recognition of her emotional labor** — she carries more and tends to not notice her own exhaustion until it's too late

## Parental Teamwork

### Complementary Roles
Natascha's assessment: "Idan brings order, structure, and stability; Sveta brings warmth, empathy, and attunement. The combination creates a balanced parenting system that gives Maya security and a stable emotional foundation."

The challenge: when out of sync, Maya experiences a double message and doesn't know what to expect. She then tests both parents repeatedly to find the boundary.

### Unified Front Protocol
1. Agree on 2–3 core non-negotiables in advance (morning routine, bedtime, candy rule)
2. If one parent sets a limit, the other backs it — even if they'd have decided differently
3. Disagreements = private conversation after Maya is asleep
4. Weekly 10-minute check-in: "What are we holding? What are we softening? What's working?"

### When Idan Is Away
- Sveta solo = harder mornings, higher exhaustion, need for extra structure
- Maya misses Idan and may express it as regression or school resistance
- Countdowns and visual markers ("3 days until Dad") reduce uncertainty significantly
- Sveta should reduce expectations of herself — solo with baby + ADHD 6-year-old is a genuinely hard load
