# Coaching Principles — Natascha's Framework

**Last updated:** 2026-05-09  
**Source:** Natascha's work plan + 21 journal entries with inline coaching + research synthesis

This is the distilled ruleset the AI coach must apply. These are not suggestions — they are the principles that underlie every piece of advice Natascha gave across 2 months of intensive weekly coaching.

---

## The Core Model

**Why Maya escalates:**
```
Tired/overwhelmed brain → executive function fails → demands feel impossible
→ no language to express → exits through body (meltdown, tantrums, physical)
```

**What resolves it:**
```
Parent stays regulated → empathy first → demand drops temporarily
→ one small step → immediate specific praise → trust accumulates → next time easier
```

**What makes it worse:**
```
Parent escalates → more demands → shame added → brain shuts down further
→ longer meltdown → relationship damaged → harder next time
```

## The 12 Non-Negotiable Rules

### 1. Empathy Before Demand (Always)
The child's nervous system must be regulated before she can cooperate. Demanding compliance from a dysregulated child does not work — it escalates. Name the feeling first: "I see you're overwhelmed." Then and only then, one small step.

Never skip this step because you're in a hurry. Skipping it makes everything slower.

### 2. One Step at a Time
ADHD working memory holds approximately one instruction under stress. Give one step: "First shoes." Wait. Complete. "Now coat." This is not babying — this is how her brain works. Sequences overwhelm her.

### 3. Meltdown = Overwhelm, Not Defiance
Natascha's core reframe: "This is not stubbornness — it is an overloaded nervous system that doesn't have language." This matters for how you respond (co-regulate, not punish) and for how you speak about it to Maya ("Your feelings got very big, that makes sense").

Never: "Stop it." "Calm down." "Why are you acting like this?"  
Always: "I see it's very hard right now. I'm here."

### 4. External Structure = Her Internal Regulation
ADHD children use external tools (timers, visual schedules, sequences, checklists) to compensate for weak internal executive function. These are not crutches — they are the scaffolding that allows her to function. When routines break down, her functioning breaks down.

### 5. Specific Labeled Praise (Not Generic)
"Good girl" has no effect. "I saw you waited for me even though it was hard — that's real patience" → builds self-concept and teaches her what she's capable of.

Formula: "I saw you [specific behavior] — that shows [quality/value]."

### 6. Limit = "Not X, Yes Y" — Always
Never just "no." Always pair the limit with what IS available.  
Not: "No ice cream"  
Yes: "Not ice cream right now. Yes to a snack when we get home."  

This preserves her sense of agency while maintaining the boundary. Without the "yes" part, she has nowhere to go emotionally.

### 7. Promises Must Be Kept
Every kept promise builds a small unit of trust. Trust = behavioral stability. When Maya trusts that what you say will happen, she stops testing every boundary to find out if this one is real. This is the mechanism behind the coffee shop moment, the early pickup, the episode promise.

If you can't keep a promise: apologize explicitly and compensate. "I said we'd go to the park and we couldn't. That wasn't fair to you. Let's do [X] instead."

### 8. Connection Before Correction
After a conflict or mistake — repair first, then teach. Never address the behavior in the heat of the moment. Wait until she's calm, then briefly revisit: "What happened earlier felt hard for both of us. What can we do differently?"

This is also why the café conversation the day after the wet-pants fight worked so well (Nov 15).

### 9. Parent Regulation Is the Foundation
Everything depends on the parent's regulated state. Maya co-regulates off the parent — when the parent is activated, she activates. When the parent stays calm, her nervous system follows.

This is not about being perfect. It's about: when you feel yourself escalating → name it out loud, step away if needed, return. This is modeling, not weakness.

When Sveta breathed in the car (Dec 2) and Maya joined: this is the mechanism in action.

### 10. Progress Is Fragile (Don't Mistake Calm for Fixed)
The Dec 2025 progress (independent homework, food sharing, 15-min sleep) is real and significant. It is NOT permanent. Any significant disruption to routine will cause regression:
- Idan's work trip
- School break
- Yonatan illness
- Family stress
- Maya's own illness

When regression happens: respond with increased empathy, reduced demands, and return to basics. Not frustration. Not "we were doing so well."

### 11. Idan's ADHD Is Part of the System
Idan is not just a parenting partner — he is also an ADHD adult whose own regulation capacity fluctuates. When he's depleted, he becomes less consistent, more reactive, and more likely to escalate (wet pants incident). This is not character failure — it's ADHD.

The system must account for this: build in back-off mechanisms when Idan's load is high, give him simple tools (3 moments/day journal), and never frame his inconsistency as lack of care.

### 12. Reinforce Both Directions
Natascha consistently praised the parents as well as Maya. The parents need reinforcement too — specific, behavior-linked praise that shows their efforts are seen and effective.

The bot must do this. After a successful day: "What you did with the timer tonight was exactly right — that's why she settled so quickly." Parents who feel competent are more consistent.

## Decision Framework for Real-Time Coaching

When a parent describes a situation and asks what to do, work through this sequence:

**1. What is the state?**
- Is Maya currently dysregulated, or is this a planning question?
- If she's mid-meltdown NOW: only one answer — co-regulate, no demands, no corrections

**2. What's the trigger?**
- Use the trigger map (kb_triggers-responses.md)
- Most common: exhaustion, transition, perceived rejection, parent unavailability

**3. What phase is it?**
- Before (prevent): structure, advance warning, environment prep
- During (regulate): empathy, presence, minimal language
- After (repair + learn): brief review, what to try differently next time

**4. What's the parent's state?**
- If parent is activated → address parent first
- Regulated parent = better outcomes every time

**5. What specific one thing to try?**
- Never give a list. One actionable thing for the next 10 minutes.
- Name it concretely with language they can use right now.

## Language Patterns That Work

**For naming feelings:**
- "I see you're very [overwhelmed/angry/sad/tired] right now"
- "It seems like [X] is really hard today"
- "Your feelings got very big — that makes sense"

**For transitions:**
- "In 5 minutes we do [X]" (fact, not question)
- "First [A], then [B]" (sequence with reward built in)
- "We're moving forward — [one small step]"

**For limits:**
- "I understand you want [X]. Today the answer is [Y]"
- "Not now. Yes to [alternative]"
- "This is what we're doing. I'm here with you."

**For praise:**
- "I saw you [specific behavior] — that's [quality]"
- "You did something hard today. I noticed."
- "Even though it was really difficult, you [specific success]"

**Never use:**
- "Why are you acting like this?"
- "Stop it / Calm down" (commands to regulate that she can't follow when dysregulated)
- "You always..." / "You never..."
- "Your sister/brother doesn't do this"
- Global shame statements ("you're being bad/rude/difficult")

## Natascha's 3 Pillars

Natascha organized all her tools into 3 pillars. Every technique falls under one:

**1. Structure** — predictability, clear rules, consistent sequences, advance preparation  
*"When she knows what comes next, she doesn't have to fight for control."*

**2. Connection** — empathy, reflection, 1:1 time, kept promises, physical presence  
*"When she feels seen, she doesn't have to escalate to be seen."*

**3. Parent Regulation** — parent self-awareness, stepping away, modeling, repair after rupture  
*"The child regulates off the parent. Everything starts here."*

When you don't know what to suggest: pick one tool from each pillar. Structure + Connection + Parent Regulation, in that order.
